# Agriculture in Brazil — Summary

## Definition / TL;DR

Encyclopedic overview of Brazilian agriculture from English Wikipedia. Brazil is one of the world's largest agricultural producers and exporters: second-largest grain exporter (19% market share, 2024) and a top exporter of coffee, soybeans, beef, poultry, sugar, orange juice, and tobacco (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#overview).

## Body

### Scale and economic weight

In 2022, Brazil produced 322.8 million tons of grain, including 154.6 million tons of soy and 131.9 million tons of corn. Crop value reached R$830.1 billion (~US$154 billion), and agribusiness contributed 26.46% of GDP. Cultivated land totals 96.3 million hectares (28.6% of national territory), with only 12% of the population living in rural areas (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#key-statistics-2023-2024).

See [[brazilian-agribusiness]] for the export and employment dynamics behind these numbers.

### Geographic split

The southern half is more temperate, fertile, and infrastructurally developed; the northeast is drought-prone and dominated by smallholders; the Amazon basin is forest-led with growing cocoa, açaí, and tropical-fruit exports; central Brazil's Cerrado was historically grassland and became a grain frontier after soil-science advances (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#geographic-diversity).

Regional production highlights (2018-2020):
- **South** (RS, SC, PR): 32% of national output — rice, wheat, tobacco, grapes, apples (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#south-region).
- **Southeast** (SP, MG, ES): historically largest share — coffee, sugarcane, orange; hosts 60% of agribusiness software firms (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#southeast-region).
- **Midwest** (MT, GO, MS): 46% of cereals/oilseeds in 2020 — soy, corn, cotton, sorghum (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#midwest-region).
- **Northeast**: family-farm dominated (82.9% of field labor) — coconut, cashew, cocoa, tropical fruits (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#northeast-region).
- **North** (Amazon states): cassava, açaí, black pepper, Brazil nuts; central challenge is combining farming with forest preservation (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#north-region).

### Historical inflection points

- **1549**: Jesuits arrive; structured cacao farming starts in the 1660s, depending on Indigenous and African slave labor (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#early-period).
- **1970s-1980s**: ~10 million hectares deforested for cattle; documented violence against Indigenous populations (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#1970s-1980s-deforestation).
- **1973**: [[embrapa]] founded to diversify production and open the Cerrado (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#embrapa-1973).
- **1994**: Plano Real cuts state subsidies; mechanization accelerates and the rural population drops from 20.7M (1985) to 17.9M (1995) (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#mechanization-and-modernization). Same year, [[pronaf]] launches to credit-finance family farmers.

### Family farming and food security

[[family-farming-brazil]] covers ~84.4% of rural properties and produces the majority of Brazil's staple foods (manioc 84%, beans 67%, milk 58%, pork 59%, poultry 50% — 2010 data). Yet ~27.6% of households experienced food insecurity in 2023; severe insecurity fell sharply from 8% (2022) to 1.2% (2023), lifting 14.7M from hunger (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#food-security).

### Infrastructure bottlenecks

- **Storage**: 75% of grain production capacity vs. 120% ideal; only 11% of warehouses are on-farm (vs. Canada 80%, EU 50%) (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#storage).
- **Transport**: heavy reliance on poor-quality highways; the 2006 National Plan of Logistics and Transportation underperformed due to underinvestment (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#transport).
- **Irrigation**: potential of ~29M ha but only ~2.98M ha realized after the 1998 drought; surface irrigation dominates at 59% (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#irrigation).

### Controversies

Persistent issues include deforestation feedback loops with extreme weather, historical slave and child labor, and heavy pesticide/GMO use (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#controversies).
