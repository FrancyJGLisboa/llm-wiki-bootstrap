# Brazilian Agribusiness

## Definition / TL;DR

Brazilian agribusiness is the large-scale, export-oriented segment of the country's agricultural economy. It contributed 26.46% of GDP in 2022, made Brazil the world's third-largest exporter of agricultural products in 2010 (behind the US and EU), and shifted Brazil's farm-sector employment from field labor to processing, services, and software (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#agribusiness).

## Body

### Position in world trade

Brazil was the "third largest exporter of agricultural products in the world, behind only the United States and the European Union" in 2010 (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#agribusiness). By 2024 it was the second-largest grain exporter with 19% global market share and fourth in overall grain production (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#overview).

Brazil leads global exports of coffee, soybeans, organic honey, beef, poultry, cane sugar, soybean meal, açai berry, orange juice, yerba mate, cellulose, and tobacco; second-largest exporter of cotton, corn, pork, and ethanol (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#overview).

### Yield revolution

"During the last two decades of the 20th century, Brazil witnessed a doubling of yield per acre," attributed to "input improvements (seeds, fertilizers, machinery), public policies that encouraged exports, reduced tax burden...more favorable real exchange rate, which had allowed price stability...increased Asian demand, productivity growth and reduced trade barriers" (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#agribusiness). [[embrapa]]'s research output is a key input to this.

### Trade balance (2007-2008)

- 2007 harvest: US$68.1 billion gross agricultural exports; US$57.3 billion net (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#trade-balance).
- 2008 major destinations: European Union (largest single market), China (13.2%), Netherlands (9.5%), United States (8.7%) (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#trade-balance).

### Employment shift

From 1990 to 2001, on-farm employment fell, but "overall agribusiness employment jumped from 372 thousand to 1.82 million. The number of companies grew from 18 thousand in 1994 to almost 47 thousand in 2001" (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#employment). The Southeast hosts "60% of agribusiness software companies" (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#southeast-region).

### Contrast with family farming

Agribusiness dominates export commodities; [[family-farming-brazil]] dominates domestic staples. Soybeans illustrate the split — only 16% from family farms in 2010 — while manioc (84%), beans (67%), and milk (58%) are family-farm-led (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#family-farming).

### Infrastructure drag

Agribusiness expansion is throttled by storage capacity (75% of grain production vs. 120% ideal; 11% on-farm vs. 80% in Canada) and weak transport — "Crops are immediately trucked to market via highways, mostly in poor traffic conditions at high cost" (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#storage, source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#transport).
