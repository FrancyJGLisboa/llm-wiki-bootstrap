# EMBRAPA

## Definition / TL;DR

The Brazilian Enterprise for Agricultural Research (_Empresa Brasileira de Pesquisa Agropecuária_), founded in 1973 during the military regime "with the objective of diversifying production" (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#embrapa-1973). EMBRAPA is the public R&D backbone that enabled Brazil to convert the Cerrado into a soy-and-corn frontier and to adapt temperate crops to tropical soils.

## Body

EMBRAPA was created in 1973 to support new crops adapted to Brazil's diverse regions and to push agricultural expansion toward the Cerrado — the central Brazilian savanna that was previously considered too acidic and nutrient-poor for large-scale grain (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#embrapa-1973).

Its work fed directly into the "doubling of yield per acre" that Brazil saw during the last two decades of the 20th century, which the source attributes to "input improvements (seeds, fertilizers, machinery), public policies that encouraged exports, reduced tax burden...more favorable real exchange rate...increased Asian demand, productivity growth and reduced trade barriers" (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#agribusiness).

EMBRAPA is also linked to the Green Revolution work led by Czech-Brazilian researcher Johanna Döbereiner, whose research on nitrogen-fixing microorganisms won the UNESCO Science Prize (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#green-revolution).

Mid-1970s soil mapping (the _Chart of Soils in Brazil_ began in 1953; IBGE's first national map published in 2003) underpins EMBRAPA's regional adaptation work, especially in the Midwest where oxisols "favor mechanization from soil preparation to harvest, partly because they are nutrient-poor" (source: raw/en-wikipedia-agriculture-in-brazil.md#soil-classification).
